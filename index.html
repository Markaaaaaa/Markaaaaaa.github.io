Black History Month: Martin Luthur King Jr.

What is Black History Month

Black History Month is an annual observance originating in the United States. It has received official recognition from governments in the United States and Canada, and more recently has been observed in Ireland and the United Kingdom. It began as a way of remembering important people and events in the history of the African diaspora. It is celebrated in February in the United States and Canada, while in Ireland and the United Kingdom it is observed in October.
Black History Month was created to highlight the contributions of African Americans to the United States. It pays tribute to all black people throughout all periods of American history, from slaves first brought from Africa in the early 1600s to African Americans living in the United States today.
In 1915, in response to the lack of information available to the public about black achievements, historian Carter G. Woodson co-founded the Society for the Study of Black Lives and History. In 1926, the organization declared the second week of February "Black
History Week" to honor African-American contributions to American history. Black history is rarely studied, and it was not included in textbooks until Black History Week was created.
This week was chosen because it included the birthdays of abolitionist Frederick Douglass (who wanted to end the practice of enslaving others) and former US President Abraham Lincoln. President Lincoln led the United States during the Civil War, which primarily fought for the enslavement of black people in the country. Many schools and leaders began to endorse it a week after its inception.
The week-long event officially became Black History Month in 1976, when U.S. President Gerald Ford expanded the recognition to "commemorate the oft-overlooked accomplishments of Black Americans in every area of endeavor throughout our history." Since then, the United States has celebrated Black History Month every February.

Martin Luthur King Jr.

Martin Luther King Jr. made an unparalleled contribution to Black History Month, fighting for equal rights for black people in the 1950s and 1960s.

Dr. Martin Luther King Jr. was born in Atlanta, Georgia in 1929. At the time, apartheid was the law in this part of the country. His experience of racial prejudice from an early age inspired his lifelong commitment to equality and justice for Americans of all colors. King believed that peaceful refusal to obey unjust laws was the best way to bring about social change.

Event

  • King and his wife, Coretta Scott King, lead demonstrators on the fourth day of the historic five-day march in 1965. Beginning in Selma, Alabama, where African-Americans have been campaigning for voting rights, King led thousands of nonviolent demonstrators 54 miles to the state capitol in Montgomery.
  • King inspired a crowd with one of his many speeches. He grew up in a missionary family and is considered one of the greatest speakers in American history.
  • King waves to supporters from the steps of the Lincoln Memorial in Washington, D.C., during the March on Washington. There, he delivered the I Have a Dream speech, which bolstered public support for civil rights.
  • On December 10, 1964, King received the Nobel Peace Prize from Gunnar Jain, chairman of the Nobel Prize Committee, in Oslo, Norway.
